\title{Imagine-Then-Act Chunk Selection for Lightweight Vision-Language-Action Policies}

\author{Author Names Omitted for Anonymous Review. Paper-ID [add your ID here]}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Lightweight vision-language-action (VLA) policies make language-conditioned
robot control more accessible, but a single predicted action chunk can commit
the robot to a poor local choice before any semantic feedback is available. We
study a test-time wrapper for frozen lightweight VLA policies that separates
three mechanisms: proposing diverse action-chunk candidates, selecting among
them before execution, and measuring final task success with the environment.
The wrapper generates a portfolio of subgoal- and prompt-conditioned candidate
chunks, executes them only through the frozen VLA policy, and evaluates
pre-execution choices through a simulation- or world-model-like selection
module. In an initial candidate-generation study with SmolVLA, native policy noise
reached real action-chunk inference but produced limited diversity
(\(0.048\) mean normalized pairwise \(L_2\), \(0.0012\) mean cosine distance),
whereas a template prompt portfolio produced substantially more diverse
policy-generated chunks (\(0.226\) mean normalized pairwise \(L_2\), \(0.030\)
mean cosine distance). We present this as evidence that wrapper-level proposal
generation is a necessary first step toward imagined chunk selection, not as a
claim of benchmark success improvement. The paper frames a conservative
architecture for improving frozen lightweight VLA policies while keeping the
success criterion external: final success is always the benchmark or
environment signal, never the VLM or simulator selector itself.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Vision-language-action models have expanded the scope of language-conditioned
robot control by mapping images and instructions directly to robot actions.
Large VLA systems such as RT-2, OpenVLA, Octo, and \(\pi_0\) connect broad
visual-language representations to manipulation policies
\cite{zitkovich2023rt2,kim2024openvla,octo2024,black2024pi0}. At the same
time, practical deployment on low-cost arms, consumer GPUs, and lab-scale
iteration loops motivates smaller policies such as SmolVLA
\cite{shukor2025smolvla}. These lightweight policies are attractive because
they reduce the cost of experimentation and deployment, yet their compactness
also makes test-time robustness especially important: a single action chunk may
encode an ambiguous grasp, a premature placement, or a trajectory that does not
match the user's intended subgoal.

The usual path to improving a VLA policy is to collect more data or fine-tune
the model. This work studies a complementary path: keep the lightweight VLA
frozen, and add an external test-time layer that makes the policy's action
choices more inspectable before committing them to the environment. The core
idea is simple. Instead of asking a frozen VLA for one action chunk, an
external module proposes a small portfolio of subgoal or prompt variants. The
same frozen VLA then produces a candidate action chunk for each variant. A
selector evaluates the imagined consequence of each candidate, using a
simulator clone, a learned visual dynamics model, or a vision-language judge,
and commits only the selected chunk to the actual environment. Final task
success remains the benchmark success flag.

This framing is intentionally different from using a VLM as a success detector
or from claiming a new model-predictive control algorithm. Our contribution is
to organize a wrapper around a frozen lightweight VLA so that candidate
proposal, candidate selection, and benchmark success remain separate
quantities. This separation matters empirically. A naive best-of-\(N\) system
requires candidate diversity, but an initial study showed that native
SmolVLA noise produced action chunks that were still very similar. In contrast,
a template prompt portfolio produced meaningfully larger action-chunk
separation while still routing every candidate through the real SmolVLA policy.
This suggests that wrapper-level proposal generation, rather than native policy
noise alone, is the appropriate object of study for test-time chunk selection.

Figure~\ref{fig:overview} summarizes the proposed architecture. A portfolio
generator expands an ambiguous task into candidate subgoals or prompts; the
frozen VLA executor produces candidate chunks; a simulation- or
world-model-like module predicts or checks candidate outcomes; and a selector
chooses a chunk for execution. The loop can be repeated when the benchmark
state indicates partial progress or failure, but the evaluation criterion is
unchanged: only environment success is reported as task success.

The resulting paper asks a bounded question: can an external wrapper improve
the usefulness of frozen lightweight VLA action proposals before any
fine-tuning or model replacement? We make three contributions toward that
question. First, we formulate imagined action-chunk selection as a modular
wrapper around frozen lightweight VLA execution. Second, we show that
subgoal/prompt portfolios can induce substantially more diverse SmolVLA action
chunks than native noise. Third, we define an evaluation protocol that
separates candidate diversity, selector quality, and environment-measured
benchmark success.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Visual Foresight and Model-Predictive Selection}

The idea of evaluating possible futures before execution is central to visual
foresight and model-predictive control. Visual foresight methods learn
predictive visual models and use them to select actions whose predicted images
move toward a goal \cite{ebert2018visualforesight}. PETS uses probabilistic
ensembles and trajectory sampling for model-based reinforcement learning
\cite{chua2018pets}, while CEM and MPPI-style procedures generate candidate
control sequences and rank them by a model or cost. Recent work such as VLMPC
and Prompting with the Future connects model-predictive ideas to
vision-language evaluation and digital-twin rollouts
\cite{zhao2024vlmpc,ning2025promptingfuture}. Our method is not a claim of
novelty over this line. Instead, it asks how the same candidate-selection
principle can be wrapped around frozen lightweight VLA action chunks, where
the low-level action generator is a pretrained VLA rather than a hand-designed
action sampler.

\subsection{Multimodal Action Generation}

For candidate selection to matter, the system must first generate distinct
candidate behaviors. Diffusion Policy and Diffuser produce diverse trajectories
through stochastic denoising \cite{chi2023diffusionpolicy,janner2022diffuser}.
ACT uses action chunking for imitation learning on low-cost robot hardware
\cite{zhao2023aloha}. Behavior Transformer and Implicit Behavioral Cloning
model multimodal behavior distributions through learned action modes or
implicit energy functions \cite{shafiullah2022bet,florence2021ibc}. These
methods build diversity into the policy or planner itself. In contrast, we
study a frozen VLA and create diversity externally, through subgoal and prompt
portfolios that still require the VLA to generate every executable action
chunk.

\subsection{Language and Vision-Language Reasoning for Robotics}

Language-model and vision-language systems often improve robot behavior by
reasoning over skills, subgoals, or affordances rather than directly producing
low-level motor commands. SayCan grounds language-model plans in learned skill
affordances \cite{ahn2022saycan}; Inner Monologue incorporates environmental
feedback into language-conditioned planning \cite{huang2022inner}; Code as
Policies converts language instructions into executable robot programs
\cite{liang2022code}; and VoxPoser composes 3D value maps for language-guided
manipulation \cite{huang2023voxposer}. Our wrapper uses a similar separation
between semantic reasoning and low-level control, but the interface is an
action-chunk portfolio for a frozen VLA. A VLM may generate candidate subgoals
or score imagined outcomes, but it is not the robot policy and does not define
task success.

\subsection{Vision-Language-Action Policies}

VLA policies connect language-conditioned perception to robot action. RT-1
scaled transformer policies on robot data \cite{brohan2022rt1}, and RT-2
showed that vision-language models can transfer web knowledge to robotic
action prediction \cite{zitkovich2023rt2}. OpenVLA released an open-source VLA
trained on a large mixture of robot demonstrations \cite{kim2024openvla}, and
Octo provided an open generalist policy across embodiments \cite{octo2024}.
Recent systems such as \(\pi_0\) and FAST explore continuous flow-based action
generation and efficient action tokenization for high-rate robot control
\cite{black2024pi0,pertsch2025fast}. SmolVLA pushes this direction toward
smaller and more accessible models, with a 450M-parameter architecture,
asynchronous inference, and action chunking \cite{shukor2025smolvla}. This
work keeps such a lightweight VLA frozen and studies how a test-time wrapper
can structure its candidate action chunks before execution.

\section{Method}

The proposed wrapper has four modules. The portfolio generator receives the
current observation and task instruction and produces candidate subgoals or
prompt variants. The frozen VLA executor maps each candidate prompt to an
action chunk. The imagination module predicts or checks the consequence of
each chunk, using either a simulator clone, a learned visual dynamics model, or
a visual judge over predicted outcome images. The selector chooses one chunk
for execution in the benchmark environment.

The architecture deliberately exposes two interfaces. The proposal interface
controls how candidate chunks are generated. This can include template prompt
portfolios, external VLM-generated subgoals, or structured perturbations around
the nominal VLA chunk. The selection interface controls how candidate chunks
are ranked before commitment. This can include oracle simulator rollouts for
upper-bound analysis, non-oracle proxy checks, or image-based VLM scoring. In
all cases, the committed action is still produced by the frozen VLA policy.

\section{Evaluation Protocol}

The evaluation separates three outcomes. Candidate diversity measures whether
the proposal interface creates meaningfully different action chunks. Selector
quality measures whether the pre-execution selector chooses candidates that
lead to better predicted or simulated outcomes than random selection.
Benchmark success measures whether the committed policy execution solves the
task according to the environment success condition. Only the third quantity is
reported as task success.

\section{Limitations}

The current formulation adds test-time computation by evaluating multiple
candidate chunks before execution. Simulator-based selection is also an
upper-bound or proxy mechanism unless replaced by a deployable learned dynamics
model. Prompt portfolios can increase action diversity, but diversity alone
does not imply better selection or higher success. Finally, because the base
VLA is frozen, the wrapper can only steer among behaviors that the policy can
already express under some prompt or subgoal context.

\section{Conclusion}

Imagine-then-act chunk selection provides a structured way to study test-time
agentic wrappers for lightweight VLA policies. By separating proposal
generation, pre-execution selection, and environment-measured success, the
framework avoids treating an auxiliary VLM or simulator score as task success.
Initial candidate-generation evidence suggests that prompt portfolios can
produce more diverse frozen-policy action chunks than native policy noise,
motivating selection mechanisms that evaluate imagined outcomes before
committing actions to the environment.
